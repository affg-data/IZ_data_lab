# U.S. Inflation Decomposition: Understanding What Drives Inflation

## Overview

Inflation does not move on its own. It is influenced by changes in energy prices, food costs, economic conditions, and monetary policy.

This project breaks down U.S. inflation since the late 1950s by separating the major components of the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and placing them within the broader macroeconomic environment.

The visualization highlights how inflation has evolved across multiple business cycles, recessions, oil shocks, and Federal Reserve policy changes.

The stacked bars decompose **Headline CPI inflation** into its major components:

- 🔵 **Core CPI** – Inflation excluding food and energy. This represents underlying inflation pressures and typically changes gradually over time.
- 🟡 **Food CPI** – Inflation driven by food prices, which are affected by agricultural conditions, supply disruptions, and global commodity markets.
- 🔴 **Energy CPI** – Inflation from energy-related goods such as gasoline, electricity, and utilities. Energy prices are highly volatile and often respond to geopolitical events and oil market disruptions.

The **black line** represents **Headline CPI inflation**, which combines all CPI components.

---

## Additional Economic Indicators

To provide broader economic context, the chart also includes:

- 📉 **Unemployment (Year-over-Year Change)** – Measures whether labor market conditions are strengthening or weakening compared with one year earlier.
- 🏦 **Federal Funds Rate** – The Federal Reserve's policy interest rate used to influence economic activity and inflation.

Gray shaded regions indicate **U.S. recessions**, as identified by the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER).

Major global oil shocks are also highlighted, including:

- 1973 Oil Embargo
- 1979 Iranian Revolution
- 2008 Oil Price Spike
- 2022 Russia–Ukraine War

---

## Key Observations

Several long-term patterns emerge from more than six decades of data:

- The largest inflation spikes often coincide with major energy shocks.
- Core inflation generally drives long-term inflation trends.
- The Federal Reserve typically raises interest rates after inflation accelerates rather than before.
- Periods of aggressive monetary tightening are frequently followed by economic slowdowns or recessions.

---

## Key Takeaway

Looking across multiple decades of U.S. economic history, inflation cycles are closely linked to three major forces:

- Energy market disruptions
- Business cycle fluctuations
- Federal Reserve monetary policy

Understanding these relationships helps explain why inflation rises, how policymakers respond, and why inflation rarely follows a simple or predictable path.

## Purpose

This project visualizes the major contributors to U.S. inflation and places them within their historical macroeconomic context.

Rather than focusing solely on the overall inflation rate, the analysis decomposes inflation into its primary components while incorporating unemployment, monetary policy, recessions, and major oil shocks to illustrate the forces that have shaped U.S. inflation over the past six decades.
